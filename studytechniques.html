Active Recall

Active Recall is a study technique involving actively recalling information by testing yourself repeatedly.

Through flashcards, self-generated questions and practice tests, this study method helps your brain memorize, retain and retrieve information more efficiently.

BENEFITS

  • Enhances cognitive aspects of learning
  • Improves long-term Memory Retention
  • Promotes efficient learning and focused attention

DRAWBACKS

  • It can be time-consuming to create study materials
  • It is possible to anticipate questions and answers, mimicking passive learning.
  • Risk of studying facts in isolation without understanding their interconnections.

There are many different strategies of doing active recall. Select a method below:

BENEFITS

  • Writing your own flashcards can boost understanding and retention.
  • Versatile and can be used for various subjects.
  • Convenient for studying anywhere, anytime.

SQ3R

The SQ3R study technique is designed to help students retain and understand information effectively through note-taking.

BENEFITS

  • Improves Comprehension and Retention.
  • Encourages active reading instead of passive skimming.
  • Encourages organizing and structuring information.
  • Enhances critical thinking by prompting questions.

DRAWBACKS

  • Can be time-consuming.
  • May feel restricting for short readings.
  • Requires consistency to be effective.

SQ3R Stands for:

  1. Survey: Skim the material to get an overview.
  2. Question: Formulate questions based on headings and key points.
  3. Read: Actively read the text to find answers.
  4. Recite: Summarize what you have learned in your own words.
  5. Review: Go over the material periodically to reinforce learning.

LEITNER TECHNIQUE

The Leitner Technique combines both Spaced Repetition and Active Recall using Flashcards to enhance learning by turing it into a game.

BENEFITS

  • Prioritizes more challenging material to be reviewed more frequently.
  • Promotes deep understanding and memory retention through active recall and spaced repetition.
  • Maximizes study time.
  • Provides a structured study timeline while keeping sessions engaging.

DRAWBACKS

  • Set up can be time consuming.
  • Amount of flashcards can be overwhelming and could lead to information overload.
  • As cards are moved to different boxes, information may become disorganized.

How To

Step 1: Prepare Flashcards

Write a question or concept on one side.

Write the answer or explanation on the other.

Step 2: Set up boxes

Using 3 to 5 boxes organize your flashcards based on review frequency.

e.g Box 1: Everyday. Box 2: Every other day. Box 3: Every Week.

Step 3: Begin Studying

Start with box 1.

If you answer the question correctly then move the flashcard to the next box.

If you answer incorrectly, leave it in box 1.

Continue this method with each box until you complete all the flashcards.
